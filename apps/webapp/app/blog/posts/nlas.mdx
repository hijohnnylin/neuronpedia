If we could reliably read an AI model's thoughts, we'd be able to build safer AI systems. However, when we look inside a running model, we see huge arrays of numbers ("activations") that are difficult to understand.

**Natural Language Autoencoders (NLAs)** are models that directly _translate_ these activations into natural language, so that we can read a model's internal thoughts in plain English.

## ➡️ [**Launch NLAs on Neuronpedia**](https://www.neuronpedia.org/nla)

- [**Paper**](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/nla/index.html) by Fraser-Taliente, Kantamneni, Ong et al, [**Anthropic Post**](https://www.anthropic.com/research/natural-language-autoencoders), and [**Intro Video**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j2knrqAzYVY)
- [**GitHub**](https://github.com/kitft/natural_language_autoencoders) - Train your own NLAs (and [submit them](mailto:johnny@neuronpedia.org) to Neuronpedia)
- [**Anthropic Tweet**](https://x.com/AnthropicAI/status/2052435436157452769?s=20), [**Neuronpedia Tweet**](https://x.com/neuronpedia/status/2052439639160762879), [**LessWrong**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/oeYesesaxjzMAktCM/natural-language-autoencoders-produce-unsupervised), [**HN**](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48052537)

NLAs build on top of previous work by [Pan et al](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.08686), [Karvonen et al](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.15674), [Huang et al](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.15712) and others, with a key difference that NLAs are a pair of models trained in a mostly unsupervised manner: an Activation Verbalizer (AV) that translates activations into text, and an Activation Reconstructor (AR) that translates the text back into activations (for "scoring").

In this newsletter, we cover the NLA release, new features since the NLA release, and **🙋 opportunities to contribute**: ways that you can add value to interpretability research that don't necessarily require a ton of experience. Opportunities to contribute are not an invitation to copy paste the thing into Claude/Codex and then copy paste the results back to us. Please use the AI as a copilot, not the director.

## ⚠️ NLAs - Word of the Day: Confabulation

NLAs are not perfect - they're relatively new, and a significant shortcoming is that NLAs can **confabulate**, or produce unexpected outputs or incorrect explanations. Read the paper's [limitations](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/nla/index.html#discussion-and-limitations) section for details.

🙋 **Opportunity to Contribute**: Reliable NLA evals/benchmarks that aren't easily gameable.

## 🆕 NLAs - Generate SAE Explanations and Scores

While experimenting with NLAs, we decided to throw SAE features/vectors into them to see what would happen. Surprisingly, they generated passable explanations for some SAE features. This was super unscientific, but then again, so was the way in which penicillin was discovered, so why not. 🤷 This section should be taken with a massive block of salt, as we (Decode) have no idea why this (sometimes) works. Special thanks to David Chanin for training SAEs to experiment with earlier NLA models.

### Why This May Be Valuable / Interesting

- **Cost**: Autointerp explanations can be expensive if using a frontier model, and scoring is even more expensive. With NLA explanations, we do generation with the AV, which is a cheaper model.
- **Subjective Error**: Autointerp explanations have many "vibes-based" configs: the wording or structure of the prompt given to the model, the data you show the model (activations, logits, etc), thinking settings, etc. By contrast, AVs simply read the activations and output text.
- **Perspective**: Autointerp explanations are generally some form of asking an external model "what's the pattern in these text snippets?". The space of outputs from this differs significantly than NLA AV outputs, which are less of an answer to a question, and more "raw" translation of the activations.

We found that NLAs can do labeling of SAE latents, with no modification required.
Instead of explaining a model's activations at a specific position in a conversation, here we simply pass the SAE vector
to the NLA's activation verbalizer. This sometimes resulted in reasonable explanations matching the top activating texts
for the latent (and also sometimes matching auto-interp explanations too).

- ➡️ [**Gemma-3-27B-IT SAE Feature**](https://www.neuronpedia.org/gemma-3-27b-it/41-gemmascope-2-res-262k/10) - Try it yourself on random Gemma 27B features.

Francesco Zaffino is working on formalizing a [notebook](https://colab.research.google.com/drive/1DYXHO61ns2iDmiySYrp9VRFzYKMYItxI?usp=sharing) that improves NLA labels and shows comparisons of Neuronpedia labels with NLA labels.

### NLA Scores for Auto-Interp Explanations

<img
  src="ASSET_BASE_URL/blog/nla-score.gif"
  alt="Demonstrating two methods of scoring non-NLA auto-interp explanations on the same SAE latent as above."
/>
_Demonstrating two methods of scoring non-NLA auto-interp explanations on the same SAE latent as above._

We found that NLAs can be used for scoring non-NLA auto-interp labels. We created three new explanation scoring methods on Neuronpedia
using NLAs that can be used now:

1. `nla_reconstructor`: (Works Poorly) Pass the non-NLA feature label into the activation reconstructor to get an activation vector. Calculate cosine similarity between the resulting vector and the SAE vector. We found that with this method, the score was almost always very bad. This approach likely has some conceptual error - eg we probably need to do something to move the vector closer to the space of the model's activations.
2. `nla_verbalizer`: (Works Okay) Generate an activation verbalizer explanation, then get its text embedding vector. Calculate cosine similarity between this vector and the text embedding vector of the feature label, which we use as the score.
3. `nla_verbalizer_last`: (Works Okay+) Same as `nla_verbalizer`, except we only use the last paragraph of the activation verbalizer explanation when generating the text embedding vector. This works slightly better because the NLAs trained by kitft happened to generate three paragraph explanations, with the last paragraph about the token itself, and the first two paragraphs about greater context. But since NLA explanations will not always be structured this way, this explanation method is extremely hacky.

- ➡️ [**Gemma-3-27B-IT SAE Feature**](https://www.neuronpedia.org/gemma-3-27b-it/41-gemmascope-2-res-262k/10) - Try the new explanation scoring methods on Gemma features.
